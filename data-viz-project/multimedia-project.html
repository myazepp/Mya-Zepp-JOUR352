What Does an At-Home Abortion Look Like in 2021?
By: Lizzie Widdicombe

Abortion Pills | Photo by Women on Waves

The practice is often assumed to be dangerous, but Abigail Aiken’s data suggest that ordering abortion pills online, and inducing a miscarriage at home, is as safe as going to a clinic.

It was the year 2000 in Derry, the second-largest city in Northern Ireland. The Good Friday Agreement had gone into effect two years earlier, bringing the Troubles to an end. The city seemed to be full of hope.

But Abigail Aiken was full of dread. An academic star, she should have been focussed on the G.C.S.E.s, a set of exams that determine whether a sixteen-year-old in the U.K. will advance on a university track or end her education in high school. But as the exam date approached, Aiken’s mind kept wandering to something else: her period, which was more than a week late.

Recently, her long-distance boyfriend had come to town for a weeklong visit, which had resulted in an unplanned romantic incident. Could she have gotten pregnant after her first time? That would be just her luck. She wanted to know, one way or the other, but this was Derry, a place where everyone knew everyone else’s business. What was she supposed to do, walk into the pharmacy and ask for a pregnancy test?

A few girls from Aiken’s junior high school had gotten pregnant. Some had continued their schooling, but most had left early to raise their babies with the help of their parents. That wasn’t an option for her, she decided. She wasn’t one of those girls who dreamed of being a mother. She wanted to go to university and see the world outside of Derry.

She could get an abortion,but the procedure was illegal in Northern Ireland. To obtain one, she would need to travel to England or Scotland—which would take money, and an excuse to leave school. That wasn’t happening without her parents’ help, and she couldn’t imagine asking them. Her father was very traditional—anti-abortion, anti-premarital sex. Her mother was relatively progressive, but had warned her, years earlier, about getting into this situation. “Do not get pregnant,” she’d told her daughter. “That is one thing I cannot help you with.”

- Abigail Aiken

When her period was ten days late, Aiken stripped off her clothes and examined her body in her bedroom mirror. She’d heard about women’s skin glowing or their nipples changing color when they got pregnant. But, as far as she could tell, everything looked the same. Then, gathering her resolve, she folded her hands into a ball and brought it down—hard—against her abdomen. Punching her stomach seemed crazy, but it was all Aiken could think of. (She didn’t have access to the Internet.) Night after night, she performed this ritual until her stomach was bruised. She also stopped eating—throwing away her school lunch and making excuses to skip dinner—and exercised until the point of exhaustion, doing sit-ups and sprinting laps.

After about five days of this, her period came back. It was more than two weeks late. Aiken felt overwhelmed with relief when she saw the blood. Finally, she could focus on her studies. She aced the G.C.S.E.s. In three subjects—English Literature, German, and Chemistry—she got the best results in all of Northern Ireland.

To this day, Aiken doesn’t know if she was pregnant. But she told me that she can still recall that feeling of relief, twenty-one years later: “It was the idea that something very, very important had just happened, and that a major potential crisis had been averted.” Aiken was speaking from Austin, Texas, where she is a reproductive-health-policy researcher at the University of Texas. One of the subjects she studies is the very act that she attempted: self-managed abortion, which is when a woman ends a pregnancy without a doctor’s help. Historically, women have used many strategies for ending unwanted pregnancies: consuming noxious substances, such as the tea used in “Bridgerton,” or causing physical injury, of which the wire hanger is an infamous symbol.

Plenty of people still try to induce abortions at home in the way that Aiken did, but, for the most part, self-managed abortions have gotten a lot safer, thanks to the arrival of “abortion pills”: mifepristone and misoprostol. The first blocks the pregnancy hormone progesterone, causing the uterine lining to break down; the second causes contractions. (Women have been using misoprostol alone to induce miscarriages since the nineteen-eighties.)

In places where abortion is legal, a doctor can prescribe the pills to a woman who is up to ten weeks pregnant. In places where abortion is illegal or highly restricted, there have been more elaborate efforts to get pregnant women the pills.

In 2001, Rebecca Gomperts, a Dutch doctor and the founder of an organization called Women on Waves, chartered a ship and began sailing it to countries that had outlawed abortion, such as Poland and Portugal. Women could board the ship to obtain the pills. (The vessel was registered in the Netherlands, where abortion had been legalized, and Dutch laws were in effect on board.) A few years later, Gomperts began dispensing the pills to women in Europe through a Web site called Women on Web. Women could fill out a consultation form describing their medical history and symptoms, and, if they were in the first ten weeks of pregnancy, Gomperts and her team of doctors would prescribe the pills, which would arrive in the mail shortly afterward from a pharmacy in India. Women on Web provided instructions on how to take the pills, and maintained a help desk to address people’s questions.

Those two things together—the pills and the Web site—were transformational, Aiken recalled. “Almost immediately, it changed things in Northern Ireland.” In 2006, she came home from medical school to find the bathroom stalls in bars and cafés plastered with stickers advertising Women on Web. Activists had put them up. “It was amazing to me to see that in Derry,” she said. She imagined how the Web site would have changed her life when she was sixteen.

Eight years later, after Aiken had become a public-health researcher, she approached Gomperts at a conference and asked if the two of them could collaborate. Together, they refined Women on Web’s consultation form, adding questions about demographic data and why people were seeking out the service. The organization also followed up with the women weeks later, to track their health outcomes and feelings after having taken the pills.

In 2017, Aiken and Gomperts published a study in the British Medical Journal, examining the experiences of a thousand women in Ireland and Northern Ireland who’d performed a self-managed abortion through Women on Web. The data showed that, in terms of safety and effectiveness, ordering abortion pills through the service and taking them was comparable to getting a surgical abortion in a clinic. Only five per cent of the women had complications that required them to complete the abortion under the care of a doctor; the rest were able to successfully end their pregnancies by using the pills.

Aiken testified on the findings before the Irish parliament, prior to the country’s decision to legalize abortion for women who are within the first twelve weeks of pregnancy. She said the country’s leaders seemed surprised that so many Irish women were getting abortions in the first place.

“There is no practical way to stop the use of abortion pills in Ireland. That horse has bolted,” one member of parliament said, after listening to Aiken’s testimony. Francine Coeytaux, of Plan C, a nonprofit that shares information on how to access abortion pills, told me, “Her research is a big part of the reason that Ireland changed their laws.”

When Gomperts and Aiken’s study came out, Women on Web wasn’t available in the U.S., where abortion was technically legal. But their research showed that there was still a demand for the service in America, especially in states where in- clinic abortions were severely restricted. In 2018, Gomperts launched Aid Access, a version of Women on Web for the United States. Aiken has been tracking the data: during the first two years that the service was offered, Americans made more than fifty-seven thousand requests for abortion pills. Most of the requests came from states with strict abortion laws, like Texas. But people from blue states sought the pills, too—because they preferred the privacy of ordering abortion medication online, or because it was cheaper than a doctor’s visit.

“Cost was the main motivating factor,” Aiken said. On average, a first-trimester abortion in a clinic costs around five hundred dollars. Aid Access provides the pills for a hundred and five dollars, or less.

Abortion pills are strictly regulated in the U.S. Mifepristone is under a special F.D.A. program called a Risk Evaluation and Mitigation Strategy, which is used for dangerous drugs—meaning that the medication can only be dispensed in a clinic, doctor’s office, or hospital, and only by a doctor with a special certification, usually after some lab tests, a physical exam, and, potentially, an ultrasound. Your physician cannot call it into your local pharmacy.

But Gomperts claims that the F.D.A. allows people to import medicines for their personal use, making Aid Access legal. The F.D.A. disagrees: in 2019, the agency sent Aid Access a cease-and-desist letter, requesting that the organization “cease causing the introduction of these violative drugs into U.S. commerce.”

The agency has also periodically seized shipments of abortion pills. Gomperts responded by suing the F.D.A. for denying her patients’ “constitutionally protected right to terminate their unwanted pregnancies prior to viability.” But the suit was ultimately dismissed, since a federal judge ruled that the case was not about abortion but rather about unapproved drugs, which would fall under the jurisdiction of the F.D.A.

There has been one recent victory for the abortion-telemedicine movement: during the pandemic, the F.D.A. has temporarily lifted the requirement that the pills be dispensed in person. But reproductive-health advocates argue that the rule should be eliminated altogether, owing to the mounting evidence that the drug is extraordinarily safe. Less than one per cent of women who have taken the pills in the U.S. have reported serious adverse events, such as hemorrhage or infections requiring antibiotics.

Aiken’s research has helped to change perceptions of the pills—and of self-managed abortions in general. The practice is often assumed to be dangerous, but her data continue to suggest that, when pills are used, inducing an abortion at home is as safe as going to a clinic.

Aiken said, “There are people out there who are very rightly questioning, If these pills are safe, why do I have to go through all these hoops to get them?” It’s more than just a matter of making them widely available online. “Why aren’t they available over the counter? Why can’t I keep them in my bathroom cabinet?”

This is the vision that Plan C is promoting. Coeytaux, who fought for years to make the emergency-contraception medication Plan B available over the counter, sees abortion pills following a similar trajectory to the morning-after pill. “They could be on the continuum,” she said, where Plan A is contraception and Plan B is pregnancy prevention.

Some people might prefer to think of the medication as “missed-period pills.” If your period is late, you take the pills to bring it back, without ever having to see a doctor—or even knowing if you were pregnant. This isn’t the norm yet, but, thanks to Aid Access, it’s already possible in the United States. Gomperts began advance-prescribing abortion pills soon after the Texas law S.B. 8 went into effect.

In the meantime, abortion advocates see the U.S. moving toward a two-tier system. In blue states, the pills will be one option among many. In red states, they’ll fill the gaps for people who have no other option. In the U.S., as in Ireland, restrictive laws have had the unintended effect of drawing attention to abortion pills. Coeytaux told me, “In many ways, we’ve made more progress toward self-managed abortion in the last two years than we could have otherwise, because of these egregious laws.” She said that the country is lucky to have Aiken “in the belly of the beast,” as Texas continues to be the center of the abortion wars. “The most world-renowned person working on data on safe abortion now is literally a Texan,” Coeytaux said.

Aiken told me it’s no coincidence that she wound up in the state. “I was interested in being somewhere where reproductive rights are important,” she said. (She has testified at the Texas statehouse, although her findings have yet to make the impact here that they did in Ireland.) But the connection goes deeper. She’s married to a Texan statistician—they’ve collaborated on research projects—and she has grown fond of her new home.

“I actually love Texas,” she said. “It reminds me of Northern Ireland. The beautiful landscape, the ruralness. People here are like the people in Northern Ireland. They’re very friendly, at least on the face of it.” She gets the occasional raised eyebrow when she tells people what she does for a living, but she’s never been harassed.

She’d almost forgotten about her sixteen-year-old self, the teen-ager who had the pregnancy scare in Derry. Then, two years ago, she got pregnant. This time, she was thirty-five. She wasn’t necessarily planning on having kids. “I was still in the boat of ‘I’m probably never going to do this,’ ” she said. But, after talking it over with her husband, she made a different decision.

Her son is now two. “He’s wonderful,” she said. She added that the prospect of motherhood feels different—more moving and powerful—when it’s a choice rather than something that’s forced upon you. That’s the flip side of abortion rights. “It’s a shame that’s not appreciated by more anti-abortion activists,” she said. “Parenthood can only be a choice if abortion is allowed.”
